Charlotte Perkins Gilman

"Charlotte in 1884, the year she married Walter"[4]

Charlotte Gilman was a writer in the nineteenth and twentieth century. She's consider to be one of the first feminist writers. Some of her most notable works are, "The Yellow Wallpaper", "Women and Economics" and "When I Was A Witch". Cynthia writes in the book “Charlotte Perkins Gilaman: A Biography.” “Charlotte as a teenager was described to be “bristled with energy.” She often considered herself sensitive, often loafing and lazy”[1(36)]. Charlotte was a free spirit who wanted to do whatever she could think of. “In her teenage years she took a resistance to house work She hated the double standard of men not having to do any of the housework”[1]. Charlotte ignored the standard she thought it would be better to just run over it. “In her own life, she refused to accept domestic confine-ment and took “a fancy to work hard and be very smart” in order to escape”[1]

As Charlotte matured she would often write about her “life mate” Martha. Martha and Charlotte shared similarities in inspiration for writing. Martha produced several literary writings including textbooks and educational materials [1] Charles Walter Stetson, Charlotte's first husband pictured her as a, “sweet little body, full of energy, and spunk” [1]. Martha was married and often described disliking marital confinement.

Charlotte followed after Martha, “she too wanted to prove that she could love and be loved by another”[1] She soon married her first husband Charles Walter Stetson, who was a self taught painter. Charles specialized in color painting, his paintings used bright and vivid colors. Charlotte found herself wanting to leave the marital life though.

As Helen describes in the book, “Making of ‘The Yellow Wall-Paper’”. “When subsequently Charlotte told him that her love for him had unaccountably evaporated” [2] Charlotte lost her love for Walter, she no longer wanted to be controlled by the victorian marital laws of the time. She would often find herself reading about the laws and divorce in articles talking about women, and marriage [2]. Charlotte for some time didn’t live with Walter as he would travel for work. She liked being on her own it gave her a sense of independence. Charlotte and Walter eventually begin to rekindled their love for one another. We even see them referring to each other as King and Queen in the letters they sent to one another [2].

Charlotte began to have the want to fulfill her “destiny” as a women[1]. She felt that she needed to bear a child and did so with Walter after a few years of marriage. This is also the time where her mental decline began. During the Victorian era women were commonly “stricken” with postpartum diseases, postpartum depression being the most common [3]. As Charlotte came near to her due date she began to become depressed. She wrote “Ambitions sleeps. I make no motion but just live”[1]. Charlotte gave birth to their daughter Katharin on March 23, 1885.

Charlotte’s mental health after giving birth to Katharin maintained its path of descension. Charlotte was later diagnosed with “nervous prostration” [2]. She was treated by Dr. Keller and was decided for her to go live with a close friend in California. She would later recover from the “illness” in 1888. In 1890 Charlotte would go on to write “The Yellow Wallpaper”.
